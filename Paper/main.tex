\title{An Explicit Evaluation of a Fibonacci Character Sum for Primes of Full Rank of Apparition}

\author[aff1]{Majid Ghandali}

\address[aff1]{Independent Researcher, Iran}

\begin{abstract}
Let $p$ be an odd prime and let $\chi_p$ denote the quadratic character modulo $p$. The \emph{rank of apparition} $\alpha(p)$ is the least positive integer $m$ such that $p \mid F_m$, where $(F_n)$ is the Fibonacci sequence. We prove that if $\alpha(p)=p-1$, then necessarily $p\equiv 3\pmod 4$ and $p\equiv \pm1\pmod 5$, and moreover
\[
\sum_{n=1}^{p-1}\chi_p(F_n)=
\begin{cases}
+1,& p\equiv 11\pmod{20},\\
-1,& p\equiv 19\pmod{20}.
\end{cases}
\]
The proof first shows that, in this regime, the quadratic nonresidue root of $x^2-x-1$ in $\F_p$ is a primitive root. This reduces the Fibonacci character sum to the structural identity
\[
\sum_{n=1}^{p-1}\chi_p(F_n)=\chi_p(\alpha-\beta),
\]
where $\alpha,\beta$ are respectively the nonresidue and residue roots of $x^2-x-1$. The sign is then determined explicitly through a discriminant criterion involving fifth roots of unity. The formula was verified computationally for all $26{,}407$ primes $p<2\times10^6$ satisfying $\alpha(p)=p-1$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Fibonacci sequence $(F_n)_{n\ge0}$, defined by
\[
F_0=0,\qquad F_1=1,\qquad F_{n+1}=F_n+F_{n-1},
\]
has long played a central role in elementary and algebraic number theory. For a prime $p$, two classical invariants govern the sequence modulo $p$: the Pisano period $\pi(p)$ and the rank of apparition $\alpha(p)$, the least positive integer $m$ such that $p\mid F_m$. These invariants go back to Lucas and have been studied extensively; see, for instance, \cite{Lucas1878,Wall1960,Renault1996,Vajda1989,Koshy2001}.

It is classical that for an odd prime $p\neq 5$,
\[
\alpha(p)\mid p-\chi_p(5),
\]
where $\chi_p$ is the Legendre symbol; see \cite{Lemmermeyer2000} for general background on quadratic reciprocity. Thus in the split case $\chi_p(5)=1$, equivalently $p\equiv\pm1\pmod5$, the maximal possible rank is $\alpha(p)=p-1$.

In this paper we study the Fibonacci character sum
\[
S(p):=\sum_{n=1}^{p-1}\chi_p(F_n).
\]
Character sums and congruences involving Fibonacci numbers have been investigated from several perspectives; see, for example, \cite{Williams1982,Sun1992,Sun2003}.

Exact evaluations of character sums attached to linear recurrences are comparatively rare. A priori, one might expect the values of $S(p)$ to exhibit substantial oscillation as $p$ varies, making a complete closed-form evaluation difficult. The main theorem of this paper shows that in the extremal regime $\alpha(p)=p-1$, this apparent complexity collapses to a single quadratic character attached to the two roots of $x^2-x-1$. Computational evidence (see \Cref{sec:computations}) suggests further regularities of $S(p)$ outside this regime, which lie beyond the scope of the present paper.

\begin{theorem}[Main theorem]
\label{thm:main}
Let $p\ge7$ be a prime such that $\alpha(p)=p-1$. Then $p\equiv 11$ or $19\pmod{20}$, and
\[
S(p)=\sum_{n=1}^{p-1}\chi_p(F_n)=
\begin{cases}
+1,& p\equiv 11\pmod{20},\\[2mm]
-1,& p\equiv 19\pmod{20}.
\end{cases}
\]
\end{theorem}

The hypothesis $\alpha(p)=p-1$ singles out a sparse class of primes. Among the $148{,}933$ primes below $2\times10^6$, our computations identified $26{,}407$ examples satisfying this hypothesis, and all agree with the formula above.

\subsection{Outline of the proof}

The proof breaks naturally into three steps.

\paragraph{Step I --- Primitivity.}
We show that $\alpha(p)=p-1$ forces $p\equiv 3\pmod 4$ and that the nonresidue root of $x^2-x-1$ is a primitive root of $\F_p^\times$ (\Cref{lem:G}).

\paragraph{Step II --- Structural identity.}
Using Binet's formula and the primitive-root structure from Step~I, the substitution $u=\alpha^n$ converts the index variable into a traversal of $\F_p^\times$ and produces the identity
\[
S(p)=\chi_p(\alpha-\beta),
\]
where $\alpha,\beta$ are the nonresidue and residue roots of $x^2-x-1$ (\Cref{prop:structural}).

\paragraph{Step III --- Explicit sign.}
We determine $\chi_p(\alpha-\beta)$ by relating the two roots to a discriminant criterion for fifth roots of unity (\Cref{lem:R2,lem:R3}).

\subsection{Related literature}

The arithmetic of Fibonacci-like sequences modulo primes remains an active area of research. Classical and recent work has investigated rank, period, and order structures for Lucas sequences \cite{BallotElia2007,SomerKrizek2014,FiebigMbirikaSpilker2025}, $p$-adic behavior and limiting densities of recurrence sequences \cite{BragmanRowland2025}, and arithmetic representability questions \cite{HajduTijdeman2025}. The present work complements these directions by providing a closed-form evaluation of a specific character sum in an extremal regime, rather than asymptotic or density-theoretic results.

To our knowledge, the explicit evaluation in \Cref{thm:main} has not previously appeared in the literature.

\Cref{sec:preliminaries} fixes notation. \Cref{sec:structure} establishes the primitivity result (Step~I). \Cref{sec:character-sums,sec:structural-identity} together carry out Step~II. \Cref{sec:sign} carries out Step~III. \Cref{sec:main-proof} assembles the main theorem. \Cref{sec:computations} reports the computational verification, and \Cref{sec:conclusion} concludes with open questions.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:preliminaries}

Throughout, $p$ is an odd prime with $p\neq5$. We write $\F_p=\Z/p\Z$ and $\F_p^\times=\F_p\setminus\{0\}$. The quadratic character modulo $p$ is
\[
\chi_p(a)=
\begin{cases}
+1,& a\in \QR_p^\times,\\
-1,& a\in \NQR_p,\\
0,& a=0,
\end{cases}
\]
where $\QR_p^\times=\{u^2:u\in\F_p^\times\}$ and $\NQR_p=\F_p^\times\setminus \QR_p^\times$. We use multiplicativity, Euler's criterion, and
\[
\chi_p(-1)=(-1)^{(p-1)/2};
\]
see \cite{IrelandRosen1990}.

When $p\equiv \pm1\pmod5$, the polynomial $x^2-x-1$ splits over $\F_p$. Let $r_1,r_2$ be its two roots; then $r_1+r_2=1$ and $r_1r_2=-1$. After choosing a square root of $5$ in $\F_p$,
\[
r_1=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2},\qquad r_2=\frac{1-\sqrt5}{2},
\]
and $r_1-r_2=\sqrt5\neq 0$. The Binet formula holds in $\F_p$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:binet}
F_m=\frac{r_1^m-r_2^m}{r_1-r_2}.
\end{equation}
This follows by induction, using $r_i^2=r_i+1$.

\section{The structure lemmas}
\label{sec:structure}

Throughout this section we assume $p\ge 7$ and $p\equiv\pm 1\pmod 5$. We carry out Step~I of the proof outlined in the introduction.

\begin{lemma}[Rank--order identity]
\label{lem:rank-order}
For each of the two roots $r_1,r_2$ of $x^2-x-1$ in $\F_p$,
\[
\alpha(p)=\ord_{\F_p^\times}(-r_i^2)\qquad (i=1,2).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since $r_1r_2=-1$, we have $r_2=-r_1^{-1}$. From \eqref{eq:binet},
\begin{align*}
F_m=0
&\iff r_1^m=r_2^m\\
&\iff r_1^m=(-r_1^{-1})^m\\
&\iff r_1^{2m}=(-1)^m\\
&\iff (-r_1^2)^m=1.
\end{align*}
Thus $\alpha(p)=\ord(-r_1^2)$. Also $-r_2^2=(-r_1^2)^{-1}$, so $\ord(-r_2^2)=\ord(-r_1^2)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Primitivity]
\label{lem:G}
Assume $\alpha(p)=p-1$. Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)}]
\item $p\equiv3\pmod4$;
\item exactly one of the two roots of $x^2-x-1$ is a quadratic nonresidue, and that root is a primitive root of $\F_p^\times$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{lem:rank-order}, $\ord(-r_i^2)=p-1$ for either root $r_i$.

For (a), set $\gamma_0=-r_1^2$. Then $\gamma_0$ is primitive, so $\gamma_0^{(p-1)/2}=-1$. But
\[
\gamma_0^{(p-1)/2}=(-r_1^2)^{(p-1)/2}=(-1)^{(p-1)/2}r_1^{p-1}=(-1)^{(p-1)/2},
\]
by Fermat. Hence $(-1)^{(p-1)/2}=-1$, so $p\equiv3\pmod4$.

Since $p\equiv3\pmod4$, $\chi_p(-1)=-1$. Therefore
\[
\chi_p(r_1)\chi_p(r_2)=\chi_p(r_1r_2)=\chi_p(-1)=-1,
\]
and exactly one of $r_1,r_2$ is a residue, the other a nonresidue. We define $\alpha$ to be the root with $\chi_p(\alpha)=-1$, and let $\beta$ denote the other root.

We now prove that $\alpha$ is primitive. Set $\gamma:=-\alpha^2$. By \Cref{lem:rank-order}, $\ord(\gamma)=p-1$, so $\gamma$ is primitive.

Write $p=4m+3$ and $n=(p-1)/2=2m+1$. There is a unique $k\in\{0,\dots,2n-1\}$ with $\alpha=\gamma^k$. From $\gamma=-\alpha^2$ we get $\alpha^2=-\gamma=\gamma^{n+1}$, so $\gamma^{2k}=\gamma^{n+1}$ and hence
\[
2k\equiv n+1 \pmod{2n}, \qquad k\equiv m+1\pmod n.
\]
The two solutions modulo $2n$ are $k_1=m+1$ and $k_2=3m+2$. Their sum is $p$, hence odd, so exactly one of them is odd. Moreover
\[
\chi_p(\alpha)=\chi_p(\gamma^k)=(\gamma^{(p-1)/2})^k=(-1)^k,
\]
and the condition $\chi_p(\alpha)=-1$ forces $k$ to be the unique odd solution.

Since $k$ is odd, $\gcd(k,2n)=\gcd(k,n)$. From $k\equiv m+1\pmod n$:
\[
\gcd(k,n)=\gcd(m+1,2m+1)=\gcd(m+1,-1)=1.
\]
Therefore $\ord(\alpha)=2n/\gcd(k,2n)=2n=p-1$, so $\alpha$ is primitive.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Order of the residue root]
\label{cor:beta-order}
Under the hypotheses of \Cref{lem:G}, $\ord(\beta)=(p-1)/2$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Let $n=(p-1)/2$. Since $\alpha$ is primitive, $\alpha^n=-1$. From $\alpha\beta=-1$,
\[
\beta=-\alpha^{-1}=\alpha^n\alpha^{-1}=\alpha^{n-1}.
\]
Hence $\ord(\beta)=2n/\gcd(2n,n-1)$. Writing $n=2m+1$, we get $n-1=2m$ and
\[
\gcd(2n,n-1)=\gcd(2(2m+1),2m)=2\gcd(2m+1,m)=2.
\]
Therefore $\ord(\beta)=n=(p-1)/2$.
\end{proof}

\section{Character sum identities}
\label{sec:character-sums}

We now collect three auxiliary identities. These will combine in \Cref{sec:structural-identity} with the primitivity result of \Cref{lem:G} to yield the structural identity of Step~II.

\begin{lemma}[Standard quadratic character sum]
\label{lem:S}
For every $a\in\F_p^\times$,
\[
\sum_{x\in\F_p}\chi_p(x^2-a)=-1.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
This is classical; see, e.g., \cite[Ch.~6]{IrelandRosen1990} or \cite[Ch.~2]{BerndtEvansWilliams1998}. We include a short self-contained proof.

Let $T(a)=\sum_{x\in\F_p}\chi_p(x^2-a)$. First, $T(0)=p-1$ since $\chi_p(x^2)=1$ for $x\neq 0$. Also
\[
\sum_{a\in\F_p}T(a)=\sum_x\sum_a\chi_p(x^2-a)=0.
\]
For $a\neq0$, using $\#\{x:x^2=t\}=1+\chi_p(t)$:
\[
T(a)=\sum_t(1+\chi_p(t))\chi_p(t-a)=\sum_t\chi_p(t(t-a)).
\]
The substitution $t=aw$ gives $\sum_w\chi_p(w(w-1))$, independent of $a$. The total sum forces this common value to be $-1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Negation involution]
\label{lem:D}
If $p\equiv3\pmod4$, then $u\mapsto -u$ is a bijection $\QR_p^\times \to \NQR_p$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For $u\in\QR_p^\times$, $\chi_p(-u)=\chi_p(-1)\chi_p(u)=-1$. The map is injective and both sets have size $(p-1)/2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Two quadratic sums]
\label{lem:Q}
If $p\equiv3\pmod4$, then
\[
A:=\!\!\sum_{u\in\QR_p^\times}\!\!\chi_p(u^2-1)=0,\quad B:=\!\!\sum_{u\in\NQR_p}\!\!\chi_p(u^2+1)=-1.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{lem:S} with $a=1$ and isolating $x=0$:
\[
\sum_{x\in\F_p^\times}\chi_p(x^2-1)=-1-\chi_p(-1)=0.
\]
By \Cref{lem:D} the residue and nonresidue halves are equal, so $0=2A$.

By \Cref{lem:S} with $a=-1$ and isolating $x=0$:
\[
\sum_{x\in\F_p^\times}\chi_p(x^2+1)=-2.
\]
Again the two halves are equal under $u\mapsto -u$, so $-2=2B$.
\end{proof}

\section{The structural identity}
\label{sec:structural-identity}

We now carry out Step~II. The key observation is that the primitivity of $\alpha$ established in \Cref{lem:G} converts the index variable $n$ into a complete traversal of $\F_p^\times$ via the substitution $u=\alpha^n$. This allows the Fibonacci character sum to be rewritten as a weighted quadratic character sum over the multiplicative group, which then collapses to a single character value via \Cref{lem:Q}.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:structural}
Let $p\ge7$ be a prime with $p\equiv\pm1\pmod5$ and $\alpha(p)=p-1$. Let $\alpha,\beta$ denote respectively the nonresidue and residue roots of $x^2-x-1$ in $\F_p$. Then
\[
S(p)=\sum_{n=1}^{p-1}\chi_p(F_n)=\chi_p(\alpha-\beta).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $D=\alpha-\beta$. Since $\alpha\beta=-1$, we have $\beta=-\alpha^{-1}$. By Binet,
\[
F_n=\frac{\alpha^n-\beta^n}{D}=\frac{\alpha^n-(-1)^n\alpha^{-n}}{D}.
\]
Since $\alpha$ is primitive (\Cref{lem:G}), the substitution $u=\alpha^n$ is a bijection $\{1,\dots,p-1\}\to\F_p^\times$. Moreover $\chi_p(u)=\chi_p(\alpha^n)=(-1)^n$, so
\[
F_n=\frac{u-\chi_p(u)u^{-1}}{D}.
\]
Applying $\chi_p$ and using $\chi_p(D)^{-1}=\chi_p(D)$:
\[
\chi_p(F_n)=\eps\,\chi_p\bigl(u-\chi_p(u)u^{-1}\bigr),\qquad \eps:=\chi_p(D).
\]

If $u\in\QR_p^\times$, then $\chi_p(u)=1$ and $u-u^{-1}=(u^2-1)/u$, contributing $\eps\chi_p(u^2-1)$. If $u\in\NQR_p$, then $\chi_p(u)=-1$ and $u+u^{-1}=(u^2+1)/u$, contributing $-\eps\chi_p(u^2+1)$. Hence
\[
S(p)=\eps(A-B).
\]
By \Cref{lem:Q}, $A-B=0-(-1)=1$, so $S(p)=\eps=\chi_p(\alpha-\beta)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The zero term at $n=p-1$ is automatically included. It corresponds to $u=1$, for which $u^2-1=0$.
\end{remark}

\section{The explicit sign via fifth roots of unity }
\label{sec:sign}

It remains to evaluate $\chi_p(\alpha-\beta)$ explicitly. Although the right-hand side of \Cref{prop:structural} depends on the labelling of roots, we now show that it is in fact determined entirely by the residue class of $p$ modulo $5$, via a discriminant criterion linking the roots of $x^2-x-1$ to fifth roots of unity in $\F_p$.

Throughout this section assume $p\equiv3\pmod4$ and $p\equiv\pm1\pmod5$. Choose $s\in\F_p$ with $s^2=5$ and $\chi_p(s)=1$. Define
\[
\phi=\frac{1+s}{2},\qquad \psi=\frac{1-s}{2}.
\]
Then $\phi+\psi=1$, $\phi\psi=-1$, and $\phi-\psi=s$.

\begin{lemma}[Fifth roots of unity]
\label{lem:R0}
For $p\neq5$, there exists $z\in\F_p^\times$ with $z^5=1$ and $z\neq1$ if and only if $p\equiv1\pmod5$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If such $z$ exists, $\ord(z)=5$, so $5\mid p-1$. Conversely, if $5\mid p-1$, the cyclic group $\F_p^\times$ has an element of order $5$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Discriminant criterion]
\label{lem:R2}
Let $t=(s-1)/2$ and $\Delta=t^2-4$. Then
\[
\chi_p(\Delta)=
\begin{cases}
+1,& p\equiv1\pmod5,\\
-1,& p\equiv-1\pmod5.
\end{cases}
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
First, $t^2=(s-1)^2/4=(3-s)/2$, so $t^2+t=1$, i.e., $t^2+t-1=0$. Also $\Delta=t^2-4=-t-3$; if $\Delta=0$, then $t=-3$ and $t^2+t-1=5=0$, contradicting $p\neq 5$.

$\chi_p(\Delta)=1$ iff $Z^2-tZ+1=0$ has a root $z\in\F_p$. Such a root satisfies $z\in\F_p^\times$ and $z+z^{-1}=t$. Using $t^2+t-1=0$,
\[
z^2+z^{-2}=t^2-2=-1-t,
\]
so $(z^2+z^{-2})+(z+z^{-1})+1=0$. Multiplying by $z^2$ gives $z^4+z^3+z^2+z+1=0$. If $z=1$ this gives $5=0$, impossible; thus $z\neq 1$, and multiplying by $z-1$ yields $z^5=1$. By \Cref{lem:R0}, $p\equiv 1\pmod 5$.

Conversely, suppose $p\equiv1\pmod5$. By \Cref{lem:R0} choose $z\in\F_p^\times$ of order $5$, set $t'=z+z^{-1}$. We verify $(t')^2+t'-1=0$. Since $z^5=1$ and $z\neq1$, $z^4+z^3+z^2+z+1=0$. Dividing by $z^2$ gives
\[
(z^2+z^{-2})+(z+z^{-1})+1=0.
\]
Using $z^2+z^{-2}=(t')^2-2$, the identity becomes $(t')^2+t'-1=0$.

The polynomial $X^2+X-1$ has discriminant $5$. Since $5\equiv1\pmod4$, quadratic reciprocity \cite{Lemmermeyer2000} gives $\Leg{5}{p}=\Leg{p}{5}=+1$. Hence $X^2+X-1$ splits in $\F_p$ with roots $t$ and $-1-t$.

If $t'=t$, then $z$ is a root of $Z^2-tZ+1$, so $\chi_p(\Delta)=1$. If $t'=-1-t$, replace $z$ by $z^2$:
\[
z^2+z^{-2}=(t')^2-2=-1-t'=t,
\]
where $(t')^2=1-t'$ from $(t')^2+t'-1=0$. Thus $z^2$ is a root of $Z^2-tZ+1$, again giving $\chi_p(\Delta)=1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Sign determination]
\label{lem:R3}
With $\alpha$ the nonresidue root and $\beta$ the residue root of $x^2-x-1$,
\[
\chi_p(\alpha-\beta)=
\begin{cases}
+1,& p\equiv1\pmod5,\\
-1,& p\equiv-1\pmod5.
\end{cases}
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$\phi,\psi$ are the two roots of $x^2-x-1$ (Vieta). Compute
\[
s\phi=\frac{s+s^2}{2}=\frac{s+5}{2},\quad
\Delta=-t-3=-\frac{s+5}{2}=-s\phi.
\]
Taking characters,
\[
\chi_p(\Delta)=\chi_p(-1)\chi_p(s)\chi_p(\phi)=-\chi_p(\phi).
\]
By \Cref{lem:R2}, $\chi_p(\phi)=-1 \iff p\equiv 1\pmod 5$.

If $p\equiv1\pmod5$, $\phi$ is the nonresidue root: $\alpha=\phi$, $\beta=\psi$, and $\alpha-\beta=s$, so $\chi_p(\alpha-\beta)=+1$.

If $p\equiv-1\pmod5$, $\phi$ is the residue root: $\beta=\phi$, $\alpha=\psi$, and $\alpha-\beta=-s$, so $\chi_p(\alpha-\beta)=\chi_p(-1)\chi_p(s)=-1$.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the main theorem}
\label{sec:main-proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{thm:main}]
Since $\alpha(p)=p-1$ divides $p-\chi_p(5)$, we have $\chi_p(5)=1$, hence $p\equiv\pm 1\pmod 5$. By \Cref{lem:G}, $p\equiv 3\pmod 4$. Combining gives
\[
p\equiv 11\pmod{20}\quad\text{or}\quad p\equiv 19\pmod{20}.
\]
By \Cref{prop:structural}, $S(p)=\chi_p(\alpha-\beta)$, and by \Cref{lem:R3} this equals $+1$ for $p\equiv 1\pmod 5$ and $-1$ for $p\equiv -1\pmod 5$. Translating to classes modulo $20$ yields the formula.
\end{proof}

\section{Concluding remarks}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have established an explicit evaluation of the Fibonacci character sum
\[
S(p)=\sum_{n=1}^{p-1}\chi_p(F_n)
\]
in the full-rank regime $\alpha(p)=p-1$, showing that $S(p)=\pm 1$ with sign determined by $p \bmod 20$. The hypothesis $\alpha(p)=p-1$ singles out a sparse class of primes, frequently occurring in computational ranges: ... among the primes below $2\times 10^6$, our computations identified $26{,}407$ such examples
Several natural questions remain.

A first question, in the spirit of Artin-type primitive root problems, is whether the set of primes satisfying $\alpha(p)=p-1$ possesses a natural density. Our data are consistent with positive density, but a rigorous treatment would presumably require conditional GRH-style methods.

Outside the full-rank regime, the sum $S(p)$ exhibits further regularities suggested by numerical experiments. In particular, our data reveal a sizeable class of primes for which $\pi(p)=4\alpha(p)$ with $\alpha(p)$ odd, where $S(p)$ vanishes systematically; the structure of this class appears to be governed by $p\bmod 8$ and the $2$-adic valuation of $\pi(p)$. A systematic understanding of these phenomena appears to require techniques beyond those used here. It would also be of interest to investigate analogous identities for Lucas numbers, more general linear recurrences, and higher-order character sums. We hope to address some of these questions in future work.

\section{Illustrative examples}
\label{app:examples}

For $p=11$: since $4^2=16\equiv 5\pmod{11}$, we have $\sqrt 5\equiv 4$, giving roots $8$ and $4$, with $\alpha=8$ (NQR), $\beta=4$ (QR). Hence $\alpha-\beta=4$, a square, and $S(11)=+1$.

For $p=19$: roots $5,15$, with $\alpha=15$, $\beta=5$, $\alpha-\beta=10$, a nonresidue, and $S(19)=-1$.

For $p=31$: $\alpha=13$, $\beta=19$, $\alpha-\beta\equiv 25=5^2\pmod{31}$, so $S(31)=+1$.

Direct computations give $\alpha(59)=58$, $S(59)=-1$ and $\alpha(79)=78$, $S(79)=-1$, in agreement with the theorem.

\section{Software documentation}
\label{app:software}

The verification suite was implemented in Python using a sieve of Eratosthenes for prime enumeration, a Fibonacci walk modulo $p$ that simultaneously computes $\alpha(p)$ and $S(p)$, and three character-evaluation backends selected by the size of $p$ (QR lookup table, bitwise Jacobi symbol, modular exponentiation via Euler's criterion). Iteration is bounded by the safety cap $6(p+1)$, which majorizes $\pi(p)$.

A representative run for $p<2\times 10^6$ processed $148{,}933$ primes and verified the theorem for all $26{,}407$ relevant primes. Full source code, documentation, raw data, and the verification CSV are available in the reproducibility archive \cite{Ghandali2026}, deposited at Zenodo with DOI \texttt{10.5281/zenodo.20707467} and source at \url{https://github.com/Majid-Ghandali/fibonacci-character-sum-full-rank}.
